Critical Analysis & Reasoning Skills (CARS)

53 questions · 90 minutes · 100% passage-based · zero outside content knowledge. The single most important section for Ontario applicants — and the one that is purely a trainable skill.

Why this section is special CARS is the only MCAT section that tests no science. Every answer is in the passage or follows logically from it. That makes it the most coachable section — and for Ontario it is decisive: McMaster screens on CARS alone, and Western (Schulich) imposes a per-section cutoff that knocks out otherwise strong applicants. Treat CARS as your highest-leverage study target.

1. How CARS is built & scored

The CARS section presents nine passages of roughly 500–600 words each, followed by a total of 53 questions (typically 5–7 per passage), to be answered in 90 minutes. Every question is tied to a passage; there are no discrete (standalone) questions and no figures, equations, or experiments. You will never need to recall a fact from outside the text — and importantly, you must not use outside knowledge to override what the passage says.

Passages are drawn evenly from two domains:

Domain (~50% each)Typical topics
HumanitiesPhilosophy, ethics, literature & literary theory, art & art history, music, architecture, cultural & religious studies, dramatic arts
Social sciencesHistory, anthropology, sociology, political science, economics, psychology (as a discipline), geography, linguistics, education, population health

Each MCAT section, including CARS, is scored on a scaled range of 118–132 (midpoint 125). The four sections sum to a total of 472–528 (midpoint 500). Percentile ranks are reported alongside scaled scores. Raw correct-answer counts are converted to the scaled score through an equating process that adjusts for small differences in form difficulty — so percent-correct does not map linearly to your scaled score, but it is still the best progress metric while studying.

Mental model CARS is a closed-book logic test that happens to use prose as its raw material. The author has made an argument; the questions ask whether you understood that argument, can reason inside it, and can carry it into new situations. Your opinion is irrelevant — only the author's reasoning counts.

2. The three CARS skill types

AAMC organizes every CARS question under one of three skills. The weightings are approximate (rounded to the nearest 5%, and they vary slightly form to form), but the proportions are stable and they tell you where to put your training effort.

Skill~WeightWhat it asks
Skill 1 — Foundations of Comprehension~30%What did the author literally say and mean? Identify the main idea, author's purpose and tone, and the meaning of a word or phrase in context.
Skill 2 — Reasoning Within the Text~30%Integrate distant parts of the passage; identify the structure of an argument; distinguish claims from evidence; spot assumptions, and judge whether the support is adequate.
Skill 3 — Reasoning Beyond the Text~40%Apply the passage's ideas to a new, hypothetical situation, or evaluate how a piece of new information would strengthen, weaken, or alter the author's argument.

The single most important fact for scoring: the largest slice — about 40% — is Reasoning Beyond the Text. These questions cannot be answered by re-reading; they require you to abstract the author's principle and transfer it. Students who only "read for the gist" plateau in the low 120s. Students who consciously extract the author's argument structure — claim, reasoning, and the values driving it — score in the high 120s and 130s.

Train by skill, not just by passage After every practice question, label which skill it tested. If you miss disproportionately on Skill 3, your reading is too literal — practice paraphrasing each paragraph into a one-line "the author believes X because Y." If you miss Skill 1, you are reading too fast and importing your own assumptions.

AAMC, "Critical Analysis and Reasoning Skills (CARS) Section Overview" — skill names and ~30/30/40 weighting confirmed at students-residents.aamc.org.

3. Active reading & passage mapping

Passive reading — letting your eyes pass over the words hoping comprehension happens — is the number-one cause of low CARS scores in strong students. The fix is active reading: a deliberate, structured engagement with the text in which you are constantly asking "what is this paragraph doing?"

The paragraph-function pass

As you finish each paragraph, mentally tag its function, not just its content. Useful tags:

  • Thesis — the author commits to a position.
  • Support — evidence, example, or reasoning for a claim.
  • Counterpoint — a rival view the author raises (often to knock down).
  • Concession — the author grants something to the other side.
  • Pivot — a turn signaled by but, however, yet, although, nevertheless, on the other hand.
  • Qualification — the author narrows or hedges the claim (some, often, in part, under certain conditions).

Track the author's voice

The most testable thing in any CARS passage is the author's attitude: are they endorsing, critiquing, comparing neutrally, or ironically distancing themselves? Watch for evaluative language (unfortunately, remarkably, naïvely, rightly) and for the moment the author's own voice separates from the views they are merely reporting. A huge share of wrong answers attribute to the author a view the author was actually describing in order to reject.

The 30-second map After reading, before touching the questions, articulate two sentences to yourself: (1) the author's main point in plain language, and (2) the author's tone toward it (for / against / mixed / neutral-analytic). If you can't, you read passively — go back to the topic sentences. This map answers most main-idea and tone questions instantly and anchors you against trap answers.

Read first, or questions first?

For most test-takers, reading the full passage once, actively and then attacking the questions beats skimming the questions first — CARS questions are too dependent on the whole argument for a search-and-find approach to work. Highlight sparingly (one or two key phrases per paragraph); over-highlighting is just another form of passivity.

4. Question-type taxonomy & attack strategies

Mapping each question to its type tells you what kind of answer is allowed to be correct. Here is a working taxonomy with the attack strategy for each.

Question type (skill)Signal phrasesAttack strategy
Main idea / primary purpose (S1)"central thesis," "primary purpose," "best title"Answer from your 30-second map. Eliminate options that are true-but-too-narrow (a single paragraph's point) or too-broad (beyond the passage's scope).
Word/phrase in context (S1)"by X the author most likely means," "in context, 'Y' refers to"Ignore the dictionary definition. Substitute each option back into the sentence and keep the one that preserves the author's meaning and tone.
Detail / retrieval (S1)"according to the passage," "the author states that"Locate the line. The answer is a faithful paraphrase — not an extension. Beware options that add a comparison or cause the passage never made.
Inference (S1/S2)"implies," "suggests," "the author would most likely agree"The answer must be forced by the text — one logical step, no leaps. Reject anything requiring an assumption the author did not license.
Structure / function (S2)"the function of paragraph 3," "the author mentions X in order to"Ask what role the sentence plays in the argument (example? concession? rebuttal?), not what it says.
Argument evaluation (S2)"the author's argument assumes," "the support is weakest because"Identify the unstated premise the conclusion depends on, or the gap between evidence offered and claim made.
Strengthen / weaken (S3)"which finding would most undermine," "best support the author's view"First state the conclusion. Weaken = attacks a premise or the link. Strengthen = supplies a missing premise. Watch for "all of the following EXCEPT."
Application / extrapolation (S3)"which scenario best illustrates," "the principle would apply to"Abstract the author's principle into a content-free rule, then test each option against that rule. The right answer matches the logic, not the surface topic.
New-information / incorporation (S3)"suppose new evidence showed... how would the author respond"Hold the author's framework fixed and ask what it predicts. The answer is consistent with the author's stated commitments.
Universal move: predict before you peek After reading a stem, answer it in your own words before reading the options. A pre-formed answer makes you immune to seductive wording. Then use process of elimination — in CARS you win by deleting three flawed answers far more often than by spotting one perfect one.

AAMC, CARS Skills 1–3 question descriptions; question-type framing corroborated by Khan Academy MCAT CARS strategy materials and The Princeton Review / Kaplan MCAT CARS chapters.

5. The five classic trap answers

The CARS exam is engineered so that the most attractive wrong answer is wrong for a specific, repeatable reason. Learn the species of trap and you can name your own mistakes.

Trap 1 — Out of scope

Introduces an idea, comparison, or causal claim the passage never made. Often true in the real world — which is exactly why it tempts you. If you can't point to text that licenses it, it's out of scope.

Trap 2 — Too extreme / absolute

Uses all, never, must, proves, impossible, only. Authors of CARS passages are usually careful and hedged; an absolute answer rarely matches a qualified author. Calibrate the answer's certainty to the author's certainty.

Trap 3 — Half-right (the partial trap)

The first clause is a perfect match; the second clause sneaks in a distortion. Read the entire option. One false word makes the whole option false.

Trap 4 — Reversal / misattribution

Flips a relationship (cause and effect swapped), or assigns the author a view the author was describing in order to reject. This is the deadliest trap on tone and "author would agree" questions.

Trap 5 — Right answer, wrong question

A true, passage-supported statement that simply doesn't answer the stem. Especially common when a "function" question offers a true content statement, or a "weaken" question offers a true statement that doesn't actually attack the conclusion.

The opposite-day check When two answers feel equally good, they usually differ by exactly one word — a scope word, a tone word, or a reversed relationship. Find that word and decide which version the author actually committed to. Do not pick the answer that sounds the most sophisticated; pick the one the text forces.

Trap taxonomy synthesized from AAMC official CARS practice rationales and standard MCAT CARS prep (Kaplan, The Princeton Review, Khan Academy MCAT).

6. Timing & pacing for 53Q / 90 min

You have 90 minutes for ~9 passages and 53 questions — about 10 minutes per passage, split roughly into 3.5–4 minutes reading and ~1 minute per question. The discipline that separates 130s from 124s is not reading faster; it is not over-investing in any one passage.

CheckpointYou should be...
30 min elapsedThrough ~3 passages (~17 questions)
60 min elapsedThrough ~6 passages (~34 questions)
85 min elapsedFinishing passage 9; reserve final minutes for flagged items
  • Triage hard passages. If a passage is dense and abstract, it's fine to read it once and lock in answers rather than chase a perfect read — the questions are worth the same as any other.
  • Never leave a blank. There is no guessing penalty. With ~30 seconds left on any unfinished question, eliminate what you can and commit.
  • Flag, don't freeze. Stuck between two options after ~90 seconds? Flag it, pick your best guess, and move on. Returning later with a fresh eye is more efficient than grinding.
  • Watch the clock on the hardest passage, not the easiest. The temptation is to bank time on easy passages and then sink it all into one brutal one. Cap every passage at ~11 minutes.
Accuracy > speed, until it isn't Early in prep, slow down and read for full comprehension even if you don't finish — build the skill first. In the final 4–6 weeks, layer in timing so the pace becomes automatic. Most score gains come from fewer careless misreads, not from reading faster.

Pacing derived from AAMC section parameters (53 questions / 90 minutes); strategy corroborated by Khan Academy MCAT and AAMC official guide test-day recommendations.

7. The Ontario stakes — why CARS is decisive

For Ontario medical schools, CARS is not just one-quarter of your MCAT — at several schools it is the part that gets weighted, cut on, or used by itself. This is why we mark CARS as the priority section.

SchoolHow the MCAT / CARS is used
McMaster (Michael G. DeGroote)Uses CARS only — the other three sections are ignored in the formula. A minimum CARS of about 123 is typically required; competitive applicants cluster around 128–129. CARS is one of three weighted components alongside GPA and CASPer.
Western (Schulich)Requires the MCAT with minimum cutoffs in each section (commonly 125 per section, including CARS). Miss any one cutoff and the application is screened out regardless of the rest.
Toronto (U of T)Full MCAT considered, historically with per-section minimum thresholds.
Queen'sFull MCAT reviewed holistically (all four sections).
OttawaMCAT not required for English-stream Ontario applicants in recent cycles.
NOSM UniversityMCAT not required.
Cutoffs change yearly Every number here is cycle-dependent. McMaster's CARS weighting and Western's per-section cutoffs have shifted between cycles. Always confirm the current requirements on each school's official admissions page (or OMSAS) before relying on a threshold.

Practical takeaway: if Ontario is your target, a one-point CARS gain can matter more than a three-point gain anywhere else. McMaster will see only your CARS; Western will use CARS as a pass/fail gate. Invest accordingly.
