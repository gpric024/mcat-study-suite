Chemical & Physical Foundations of Biological Systems

59 questions · 95 minutes · AAMC Foundational Concepts 4 & 5. General chemistry (~30%), physics (~25%), biochemistry (~25%), organic chemistry (~15%), intro biology (~5%). The exam rewards applying physics and chemistry to biological and laboratory scenarios and reading data, not bare recall.

How this section actually tests you

Most questions sit inside passages that describe an experiment, a clinical scenario, or a figure, mixed with discrete standalone questions. Beyond knowing the content, you are graded on the four Scientific Inquiry and Reasoning Skills (SIRS): (1) knowledge of scientific concepts, (2) scientific reasoning and problem-solving, (3) reasoning about research design and execution, and (4) data-based and statistical reasoning. Practically: expect to manipulate equations symbolically, read graphs/tables, and judge whether a conclusion is supported.

No calculator. You must estimate. Memorize logs (log 2 ≈ 0.3, log 3 ≈ 0.48), round constants (g ≈ 10 m/s²), and use scientific notation. Answer choices are usually spread out enough that an order-of-magnitude estimate picks the right one.

1. Mechanics: Motion, Force, Work & Energy

Kinematics and Newton’s laws appear as physics discretes and inside biomechanics passages (e.g., forces on a joint, a falling object, terminal velocity). The MCAT keeps the math light but the conceptual setup is the trap.

Core equations

QuantityRelationshipNotes
Kinematics (constant a)v = v₀ + at;   x = v₀t + ½at²   v² = v₀² + 2axProjectile: treat x and y independently; vy = 0 at the top.
Newton’s 2nd lawΣF = maOn an incline, gravity splits into mg·sinθ (along) and mg·cosθ (normal).
WorkW = Fd·cosθForce ⊥ displacement does zero work (e.g., centripetal force, normal force on flat ground).
Kinetic / Potential energyKE = ½mv²   PEgrav = mghWork–energy theorem: Wnet = ΔKE.
PowerP = W/t = FvWatts. 1 hp ≈ 746 W (not needed, but units matter).
Momentum / Impulsep = mv   J = FΔt = ΔpMomentum conserved in all collisions; KE conserved only in elastic ones.
High-yield distinction Energy is conserved overall, but mechanical energy (KE+PE) is conserved only when no friction/drag/nonconservative force acts. If the question mentions friction, air resistance, or a “rough” surface, mechanical energy is lost to heat.

Worked example

A 2 kg block slides from rest down a frictionless ramp of height 5 m. Find its speed at the bottom. Energy conservation: mgh = ½mv² ⇒ v = √(2gh) = √(2·10·5) = √100 = 10 m/s. Mass cancels — a classic MCAT “the mass doesn’t matter” result.

AAMC Content Category 4A: Translational motion, forces, work, energy, and equilibrium in living systems; Khan Academy MCAT Physics.

2. Fluids & Gases in Circulation

Fluid dynamics maps directly onto the cardiovascular and respiratory systems — the AAMC explicitly frames Category 4B around blood circulation and gas exchange. Expect continuity, Bernoulli, Poiseuille, and the gas laws.

Key relationships

PrincipleEquationMCAT use
Hydrostatic pressureP = ρgh (gauge); Pabs = P₀ + ρghDepends on depth only, not container shape.
Continuity (incompressible)A₁v₁ = A₂v₂Narrower vessel → faster flow. Capillary bed has huge total area → slow flow.
BernoulliP + ½ρv² + ρgh = constFaster fluid → lower pressure (at same height).
Poiseuille flowQ ∝ r⁴ (and ∝ ΔP, ∝ 1/η, ∝ 1/L)Halving radius cuts flow 16×. Drives vasoconstriction effects.
BuoyancyFb = ρfluidVdisplacedgFloat if ρobject < ρfluid.
Ideal gasPV = nRTSTP molar volume 22.4 L. Real gases deviate at high P, low T.
Watch the exponent. Poiseuille’s resistance scales as r⁴. A small change in vessel radius produces a huge change in flow/resistance — the single most tested fluids fact on the exam.

Worked example

Blood in the aorta (area A) speeds up as it enters a stenotic (narrowed) region of area A/4. By continuity, v scales as 1/A, so velocity quadruples (4v). By Bernoulli at the same height, the faster region has lower pressure. This is why arterial narrowing can paradoxically drop local pressure.

AAMC Content Category 4B: Importance of fluids for the circulation of blood, gas movement, and gas exchange; Khan Academy MCAT Fluids.

3. Thermodynamics & Thermochemistry

Bridges physics and general chemistry. The core is the relationship among enthalpy (H), entropy (S), and Gibbs free energy (G), plus calorimetry and the laws of thermodynamics.

Master equation

ΔG = ΔH − TΔS

ΔGMeaning
< 0Spontaneous (exergonic) in the forward direction.
= 0At equilibrium.
> 0Nonspontaneous forward (endergonic); reverse is favored.
ΔHΔSSpontaneity
−+Spontaneous at all T.
+−Never spontaneous.
−−Spontaneous at low T (enthalpy-driven).
++Spontaneous at high T (entropy-driven).
Relate to equilibrium & cells. ΔG° = −RT ln K = −nFE°cell. So K>1 ⇔ ΔG°<0 ⇔ E°cell>0. These three statements are interchangeable — passages often give you one and ask about another.

Calorimetry & laws

  • q = mcΔT (heating one phase); during a phase change temperature is constant and q = mL (latent heat).
  • State functions (H, S, G, U) depend only on initial/final states; q and w are path-dependent.
  • 1st law: ΔU = q + w (chemistry sign convention: w = work done on the system).
  • Hess’s law: sum enthalpies of steps; reverse a reaction → flip the sign of ΔH.

AAMC Content Category 5E: Principles of chemical thermodynamics and kinetics; Khan Academy MCAT Thermodynamics.

4. Electrochemistry & Circuits

Category 4C couples physics circuits with galvanic/electrolytic cells — directly relevant to membrane potentials and the electron transport chain.

Circuits

LawEquationSeries vs parallel
Ohm’s lawV = IR—
PowerP = IV = I²R = V²/R—
ResistorsRseries = ΣR;   1/Rparallel = Σ(1/R)Parallel R is always < the smallest resistor.
CapacitorsC = Q/V;   Cparallel = ΣC; 1/Cseries = Σ(1/C)Capacitors add the opposite way from resistors.

Electrochemical cells

AN OX / RED CAT — always. Anode = oxidation; cathode = reduction, in both galvanic and electrolytic cells. What flips is the sign: in a galvanic (voltaic) cell the anode is negative and the reaction is spontaneous (E°cell>0); in an electrolytic cell the anode is positive and an external power source drives a nonspontaneous reaction (E°cell<0).
  • E°cell = E°cathode − E°anode (both as reduction potentials). Higher reduction potential = stronger oxidizing agent and gets reduced.
  • Nernst equation: E = E° − (RT/nF) ln Q ≈ E° − (0.059/n) log Q at 25°C. Underlies the ≈−70 mV neuronal resting potential.

AAMC Content Category 4C: Electrochemistry and electrical circuits and their elements; Khan Academy MCAT Electrochemistry.

5. Light, Sound & Optics

Category 4D covers how light and sound interact with matter: geometric optics (lenses/mirrors), the wave nature of light, the Doppler effect, and sound intensity in decibels.

Geometric optics

  • Thin lens / mirror: 1/f = 1/o + 1/i; magnification m = −i/o. Power P = 1/f (diopters, f in meters).
  • Sign conventions: converging lens / concave mirror have positive f. Real images have positive i and are inverted; virtual images have negative i and are upright.
  • Refraction (Snell): n₁sinθ₁ = n₂sinθ₂. Light bends toward the normal entering a denser (higher n) medium. Total internal reflection occurs past the critical angle going from high-n to low-n.

Waves & sound

  • v = fλ. When a wave changes medium, frequency stays constant; speed and wavelength change.
  • Doppler: approaching source → higher observed frequency; receding → lower. Basis of ultrasound/echocardiography.
  • Decibels: β = 10 log(I/I₀). Every ×10 in intensity adds 10 dB; +20 dB means intensity ×100.
Frequency is invariant across media. Students wrongly change f when light enters glass. f is set by the source; λ shrinks (λ = λvac/n) so v drops.

AAMC Content Category 4D: How light and sound interact with matter; Khan Academy MCAT Light & Optics.

6. Atomic Structure & Nuclear

Category 4E spans the photoelectric effect, atomic emission, electron configuration, periodic trends, and nuclear decay/half-life.

TopicKey fact
Photon energyE = hf = hc/λ. Shorter wavelength = higher energy.
Photoelectric effectElectrons ejected only if photon energy ≥ work function. Above threshold, KE’s ceiling rises with frequency; intensity affects number, not energy, of electrons.
Periodic trendsElectronegativity & ionization energy increase up and to the right; atomic radius increases down and to the left.
Quantum numbersn (shell), l (subshell), ml (orbital), ms (±½). No two electrons share all four (Pauli).

Nuclear decay

  • α (−2 protons, −4 mass), β− (neutron→proton, +1 atomic number, emits electron), β+ (proton→neutron, −1 atomic number), γ (energy only, no mass/charge change).
  • Half-life: after n half-lives, fraction remaining = (½)n. Decay is first-order and independent of starting amount.

AAMC Content Category 4E: Atoms, nuclear decay, electronic structure, and atomic chemical behavior; Khan Academy MCAT Atomic structure.

7. Acids, Bases, Buffers & Titration

Category 5A (water and its solutions) is one of the heaviest-tested areas and connects to blood pH, amino acid charge, and enzyme behavior.

  • pH = −log[H⁺]; pOH = −log[OH⁻]; pH + pOH = 14 (25°C). Each pH unit = 10× in [H⁺].
  • Strong acids/bases dissociate completely; weak ones reach equilibrium (Ka, Kb). pKa + pKb = 14. Lower pKa = stronger acid.
  • Henderson–Hasselbalch: pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA]). When [A⁻]=[HA], pH = pKa (the buffer’s point of maximum capacity).
Buffer = weak acid + its conjugate base. Best buffering within pKa ± 1. At the half-equivalence point of a weak-acid titration, pH = pKa. At the equivalence point of a weak acid + strong base, the solution is basic (conjugate base hydrolyzes), so pH > 7.

Titration logic

TitrationEquivalence-point pH
Strong acid + strong base= 7
Weak acid + strong base> 7 (basic)
Weak base + strong acid< 7 (acidic)

8. Kinetics & Equilibrium

Category 5E again: reaction rates, rate laws, catalysts, and the equilibrium constant — plus the biochemistry crossover of enzyme kinetics (Michaelis–Menten).

  • Rate law rate = k[A]m[B]n; orders come from experiment, not stoichiometry. The slowest step (rate-determining step) governs the overall rate.
  • Catalysts (incl. enzymes) lower activation energy and speed both directions equally — they do not change ΔG, ΔH, or Keq, only how fast equilibrium is reached.
  • Keq = [products]/[reactants] (each to its coefficient; pure solids/liquids excluded). Compare Q to K: Q<K shifts forward, Q>K shifts reverse.
  • Le Châtelier: stress (concentration, pressure/volume, temperature) shifts equilibrium to relieve it. Only temperature changes K itself.
Enzyme kinetics. V₀ = Vmax[S]/(Km+[S]). Km = [S] at ½Vmax (inverse measure of affinity — low Km = high affinity). Competitive inhibitors raise apparent Km, Vmax unchanged (overcome by more substrate). Noncompetitive lower Vmax, Km unchanged.

AAMC Content Category 5E: Principles of chemical thermodynamics and kinetics; Content Category 5D (biologically relevant molecules); Khan Academy MCAT Kinetics & Enzymes.

Spectroscopy quick reference

  • IR: functional groups. Broad ~3300 cm⁻¹ O–H; sharp ~1700 cm⁻¹ C=O carbonyl.
  • ¹H NMR: number of signals = chemically distinct H environments; integration = relative number of H; splitting = n+1 rule (neighboring H).
  • UV-Vis / Beer–Lambert: A = εbc — absorbance is linear in concentration; basis of spectrophotometric assays.
  • Mass spec: molecular ion peak gives molecular weight.
