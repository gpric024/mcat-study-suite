Psychological, Social & Biological Foundations of Behavior

How to use this guide. Nine modules cover the entire content map. Each has a focused explanation of the high-yield testable facts, a worked example or comparison table, the AAMC content-category citation, and an embedded 6–10 question practice set with full rationales. Answer the questions before revealing—active retrieval is the highest-yield study behavior (a fact this section will literally test you on).

Those modules are intentionally heavier and use a flashcard-style "term → one-line definition → classic distractor" structure, because the Psych/Soc section is overwhelmingly a definition-discrimination test, not a reasoning-from-first-principles test.

Module 1 · Sensation & Perception

Sensation is the raw transduction of physical stimuli into neural signals; perception is the brain's organization and interpretation of those signals. Keep the direction straight: bottom-up (data-driven) processing builds percepts from raw sensory features; top-down (conceptually-driven) processing uses prior knowledge, context, and expectation to interpret incomplete data.

Thresholds and psychophysics

TermDefinitionWatch out for
Absolute thresholdMinimum stimulus intensity detectable 50% of the timeNot "any detectable amount"—it is the 50% point
Difference threshold (JND)Smallest detectable difference between two stimuliDistinct from absolute threshold
Weber's lawJND is a constant proportion of the original stimulus, not a constant amountTested as a ratio, not absolute units
Signal detection theoryDetection depends on signal strength and the observer's decision criterion; yields hits, misses, false alarms, correct rejectionsSeparates sensitivity (d′) from response bias—motivation/expectation shift the criterion, not sensitivity
Sensory adaptationDecreased response to a constant stimulus over timeDifferent from habituation (a learning phenomenon)

Vision and audition (high-yield)

  • Phototransduction: rods (dim light, peripheral, achromatic, high sensitivity) vs. cones (color, central/fovea, acuity). Light hyperpolarizes photoreceptors.
  • Color vision: Trichromatic (Young–Helmholtz) theory—three cone types—explains the retina; opponent-process theory—red/green, blue/yellow, black/white antagonist channels—explains afterimages and color contrast. Both are correct at different stages. This is a classic trap: they are complementary, not competing.
  • Auditory: place theory (pitch coded by basilar-membrane location; high frequencies near oval window/base) vs. frequency/temporal theory (firing rate matches frequency; works for low pitches; volley principle extends it).

Perceptual organization

Gestalt principles (proximity, similarity, continuity, closure, figure–ground, common fate, "law of prägnanz"/simplicity) describe how we group features into wholes—the canonical example of top-down organization. Perceptual constancies (size, shape, color) keep objects stable despite changing retinal images. Weber, Fechner, and Stevens are the psychophysics names; signal detection is the framework.

AAMC Content Category 6A, “Sensing the environment” & perception topics; Khan Academy MCAT: Sensation and Perception. Trichromatic vs. opponent-process are stage-complementary (Kandel, Principles of Neural Science).

Module 2 · Cognition, Memory & Language

Memory architecture

The modal (Atkinson–Shiffrin) model: sensory memory (iconic ~250 ms, echoic ~3–4 s) → short-term/working memory (~7±2 items, ~15–30 s) → long-term memory (effectively unlimited). Baddeley's working memory adds a central executive coordinating the phonological loop and visuospatial sketchpad (plus an episodic buffer).

LTM typeSubtypeExample
Explicit (declarative)EpisodicYour first day of class (events)
Explicit (declarative)SemanticThe capital of France (facts)
Implicit (nondeclarative)ProceduralRiding a bike (skills)
Implicit (nondeclarative)PrimingFaster recognition after exposure

Encoding, retrieval, and forgetting

  • Encoding: elaborative rehearsal (deep, semantic, levels-of-processing) beats maintenance rehearsal (rote). The self-reference effect and spacing effect boost retention.
  • Retrieval cues: context-dependent (environment) and state-dependent (internal/mood) memory; the encoding specificity principle.
  • Serial position effect: primacy (LTM) + recency (STM) → better recall of list ends.
  • Forgetting: proactive interference (old disrupts new) vs. retroactive interference (new disrupts old)—a frequent swap-the-definitions trap. Decay, retrieval failure, and Ebbinghaus's forgetting curve round it out.
  • Memory distortion: the misinformation effect (Loftus), source monitoring errors, and reconstructive memory.
  • Neuro: hippocampus consolidates new explicit memories (damage → anterograde amnesia); amygdala adds emotional valence; cerebellum/basal ganglia handle procedural memory; long-term potentiation (LTP) is the synaptic substrate.

Cognition, problem-solving, and language

  • Heuristics & biases (Kahneman/Tversky): availability (ease of recall), representativeness (resemblance to a prototype → base-rate neglect), anchoring, confirmation bias, framing, overconfidence, belief perseverance.
  • Problem-solving: algorithms (guaranteed) vs. heuristics (fast); barriers include fixation, functional fixedness, and mental set.
  • Intelligence: Spearman's g; Gardner's multiple intelligences; Sternberg's triarchic theory; fluid (reasoning, declines with age) vs. crystallized (knowledge, stable/rises).
  • Language theories: Skinner (operant/behaviorist), Chomsky (nativist—language acquisition device, universal grammar), and the Whorfian linguistic relativity hypothesis (language shapes thought). Broca's area = production (non-fluent aphasia); Wernicke's = comprehension (fluent but nonsensical aphasia).

AAMC Content Category 6B, “Making sense of the environment”; Khan Academy MCAT: Cognition, Memory & Language. Working-memory model: Baddeley & Hitch.

Module 3 · Learning, Motivation, Emotion & Stress

Associative learning

Classical (Pavlovian)Operant (Skinner)
What's learnedAssociation between two stimuli (involuntary/reflexive response)Association between behavior and consequence (voluntary)
Key termsUS, UR, CS, CR; acquisition, extinction, spontaneous recovery, generalization, discriminationReinforcement (↑ behavior), punishment (↓ behavior); shaping; extinction
Classic exampleDog salivates to a bellRat presses lever for food
The reinforcement/punishment grid (memorize this) Positive = add a stimulus; negative = remove a stimulus. Reinforcement always increases behavior; punishment always decreases it.
  • Positive reinforcement: add good (give candy) → behavior ↑
  • Negative reinforcement: remove bad (turn off alarm by getting up) → behavior ↑ — not punishment, the #1 trap
  • Positive punishment: add bad (spanking) → behavior ↓
  • Negative punishment: remove good (take away phone) → behavior ↓

Reinforcement schedules: Variable-ratio (e.g., slot machines) produces the highest, most extinction-resistant response rate. Order of response rates: variable-ratio > fixed-ratio > variable-interval > fixed-interval. Other learning: observational learning / modeling (Bandura's Bobo doll), latent learning (Tolman—cognitive maps), insight learning.

Motivation

  • Drive-reduction theory: behavior reduces arousal to restore homeostasis (a physiological need creates a drive).
  • Incentive theory: behavior is pulled by external rewards.
  • Arousal theory & Yerkes–Dodson law: performance peaks at moderate arousal; the optimum is lower for difficult tasks.
  • Maslow's hierarchy: physiological → safety → love/belonging → esteem → self-actualization.
  • Self-determination theory: intrinsic motivation rests on autonomy, competence, relatedness. The overjustification effect: external reward can undermine intrinsic motivation.

Emotion

TheorySequence (after stimulus)One-line hook
James–LangePhysiological arousal → emotion"I'm afraid because my heart is racing"
Cannon–BardArousal AND emotion simultaneouslyBody and feeling fire at the same time
Schachter–Singer (two-factor)Arousal + cognitive label → emotionArousal is interpreted using context
LazarusCognitive appraisal must precede emotionAppraisal first, even unconsciously

Neuro: the amygdala is central to fear; the limbic system mediates emotion; the prefrontal cortex regulates it.

Stress

Stressors can be appraised as harm/loss, threat, or challenge (primary appraisal) followed by evaluation of coping resources (secondary appraisal). Selye's General Adaptation Syndrome: alarm → resistance → exhaustion. The HPA axis releases cortisol; chronic stress impairs immunity and the hippocampus. Coping is problem-focused vs. emotion-focused.

AAMC Content Categories 6C (“Responding to the world”—emotion, stress) and 7A (“Individual influences on behavior”—learning, motivation); Khan Academy MCAT: Learning, Motivation & Emotion. GAS: Selye (1956).

Module 4 · Personality, Psychological Disorders & Biology of Behavior

Personality theories

PerspectiveCore claimNames

Defense mechanisms (high-yield to discriminate): repression, denial, projection (attributing your impulse to others), reaction formation (acting opposite), displacement (redirecting to a safer target), rationalization, regression, sublimation (channeling into acceptable outlets), identification.

Psychological disorders (DSM framework)

  • Depressive: major depressive disorder (5+ symptoms, 2+ weeks); persistent depressive disorder.
  • Bipolar: mania (Bipolar I) ± depression; hypomania (Bipolar II).
  • Anxiety: GAD, panic disorder, phobias; (OCD and PTSD are now separate DSM-5 categories—know this reclassification).
  • Schizophrenia: the MCAT's representative psychotic disorder. Positive symptoms (added: hallucinations, delusions, disorganized speech) vs. negative symptoms (lost: flat affect, avolition, alogia, anhedonia). Dopamine hypothesis.
  • Dissociative (dissociative identity, amnesia, depersonalization) and somatic symptom disorders.
  • Personality disorders: Cluster A (odd—paranoid, schizoid, schizotypal), B (dramatic—antisocial, borderline, histrionic, narcissistic), C (anxious—avoidant, dependent, obsessive-compulsive personality).
Biology of mood The monoamine/biogenic-amine hypothesis links depression to low serotonin, norepinephrine, and dopamine. Excess dopamine activity is implicated in schizophrenia's positive symptoms. These are classic discrete-question facts.

Biological bases of behavior (the ~5% you should ace)

CNS/PNS organization; neuron and action potential; neurotransmitters (ACh, dopamine, serotonin, GABA, glutamate, norepinephrine, endorphins). Lobes: frontal (executive, motor, Broca), parietal (somatosensory), temporal (auditory, Wernicke, memory), occipital (vision). Subcortical: thalamus (relay), hypothalamus (homeostasis/endocrine), limbic system. Autonomic: sympathetic ("fight or flight") vs. parasympathetic ("rest and digest"). Methods: EEG, fMRI, PET, lesion studies.

AAMC Content Category 7A, “Individual influences on behavior” (personality, psychological disorders, biological bases); Khan Academy MCAT: Personality & Psychological Disorders; DSM-5 categories.

Attachment (Ainsworth's Strange Situation): secure, avoidant, anxious-ambivalent/resistant, disorganized. Harlow's monkeys (contact comfort > food). Temperament (Thomas & Chess). Parenting styles (Baumrind): authoritative (best outcomes), authoritarian, permissive, neglectful.

Self and identity (Content Category 8A)

  • Self-concept (the cognitive picture of who you are) and self-esteem (evaluative). Self-efficacy (Bandura)—belief you can succeed at a specific task; locus of control (internal vs. external, Rotter).
  • Identity formation: Erikson's identity crisis; Marcia's four identity statuses (diffusion, foreclosure, moratorium, achievement).
  • Social theories of self: Cooley's looking-glass self (we form our self-image from how we imagine others see us) and Mead's I and the me with the generalized other. These bridge psychology and sociology—flag them. Reference groups shape self-evaluation.
  • Identity types: gender, ethnic, national, religious identity; intersectionality.

AAMC Content Categories 7A (development) and 8A (“Self-identity”); Khan Academy MCAT: Self Identity & Development. Cooley and Mead are symbolic-interactionist; expect them on either psych or soc items.

Module 6 · Social Cognition, Attribution & Attitudes

Why this module matters for you This is dense social-psych vocabulary—the part neuroscience majors most often lose easy points on. The questions are almost always "which term names this scenario?" Drill the definitions until discrimination is automatic.

Attribution theory

  • Dispositional (internal) vs. situational (external) attributions.
  • Fundamental attribution error: overweighting disposition, underweighting situation—when explaining others' behavior.
  • Actor–observer bias: I attribute my own behavior to the situation but others' to disposition.
  • Self-serving bias: credit success to self (internal), blame failure on circumstances (external).
  • Just-world hypothesis: belief that people get what they deserve.
  • Attribution dimensions (Weiner): locus (internal/external), stability, controllability. Kelley's covariation model uses consistency, distinctiveness, consensus.

Attitudes and persuasion

  • Components of attitude (ABC): Affective, Behavioral, Cognitive.
  • Cognitive dissonance (Festinger): tension from inconsistent attitudes/behaviors drives attitude change; classic $1/$20 study—less external justification produces more dissonance and attitude change.
  • Elaboration Likelihood Model: central route (high involvement, evaluates argument quality—durable change) vs. peripheral route (cues like attractiveness/expertise—superficial change).
  • Foot-in-the-door (small request first) vs. door-in-the-face (large rejected request first).

Social perception & bias

Stereotype (cognitive belief about a group), prejudice (affective attitude), discrimination (behavior)—the ABC of intergroup bias; do not collapse them. Related: self-fulfilling prophecy (and the related stereotype threat, where awareness of a stereotype depresses performance), ethnocentrism, in-group favoritism / out-group homogeneity. Primacy and recency effects shape impression formation; the halo effect and just-world belief distort it.

AAMC Content Categories 8B (“Social thinking”) and 7C (“Attitude and behavior change”); Khan Academy MCAT: Social Psychology. Cognitive dissonance: Festinger & Carlsmith (1959).

Module 7 · Social Influence & Group Dynamics

Conformity, compliance, obedience

TermDefinitionLandmark study
ConformityMatching attitudes/behavior to group normsAsch (line judgments)
ComplianceChanging behavior in response to a direct requestFoot-in-the-door / door-in-the-face
ObedienceFollowing orders from an authority figureMilgram (shock experiment)
Identification / internalizationConforming to be like a valued group / because you accept the beliefKelman's processes

Normative influence (to be liked/accepted) vs. informational influence (to be correct). Sherif's autokinetic study = informational. Zimbardo's Stanford Prison study = power of roles/deindividuation.

Group processes

  • Social facilitation: presence of others improves performance on simple/well-learned tasks but impairs complex/novel ones.
  • Social loafing: reduced individual effort in a group (diffusion of responsibility).
  • Deindividuation: loss of self-awareness/restraint in groups (anonymity, arousal).
  • Group polarization: group discussion strengthens members' initial leanings (more extreme).
  • Groupthink: desire for harmony overrides realistic appraisal of alternatives (Janis); symptoms include illusion of invulnerability, self-censorship, mindguards.
  • Bystander effect: the more bystanders, the less likely any one helps (diffusion of responsibility, pluralistic ignorance); Darley & Latané.

Socialization, norms, and social interaction (Content Category 8C)

  • Socialization: primary (childhood, family) vs. secondary (later, institutions); agents of socialization (family, school, peers, media); resocialization in total institutions (Goffman).
  • Norms, folkways, mores, taboos; sanctions (positive/negative, formal/informal); deviance.
  • Theories of deviance: labeling theory (deviance is socially constructed by the reaction; primary vs. secondary deviance), strain theory (Merton—gap between cultural goals and legitimate means), differential association (deviance learned in groups), social control theory.
  • Goffman's dramaturgy: front stage / back stage; impression management.
  • Group structure: primary (intimate, e.g., family) vs. secondary (goal-oriented) groups; in-groups/out-groups; reference groups; networks; organizations (bureaucracy—Weber's ideal type, McDonaldization—Ritzer).

AAMC Content Categories 7B (“Social processes that influence human behavior”) and 8C (“Social interactions”); Khan Academy MCAT: Social Influence. Milgram (1963); Latané & Darley (1968).

Module 8 · Sociological Theory & Social Institutions

Highest-leverage module for a neuroscience major. Sociology is ~30% of the section and is the most common blind spot for science-heavy applicants. The four theoretical paradigms below appear constantly—both as discrete questions and as the lens a passage adopts. Memorize the macro/micro split and the one-line signature of each.

The four major theoretical paradigms

ParadigmLevelCore view of societyKey thinkersSignature phrase
Functionalism (structural functionalism)MacroSociety is an interdependent system of parts, each serving a function to maintain stability/equilibrium; manifest vs. latent functions; dysfunctionsDurkheim, Parsons, Merton"What purpose does it serve for social order?"
Conflict theoryMacroSociety is competition over scarce resources; institutions reproduce inequality and the dominance of one group over others; change comes through struggleMarx (class), Weber, Du Bois (race), feminist theorists (gender)"Who benefits, who is exploited?"
Symbolic interactionismMicroSociety is constructed through everyday interaction and shared symbols/meanings; reality is negotiated face-to-faceMead, Cooley, Blumer, Goffman"What does this mean to the people interacting?"
Social constructionismMicro/macroSocial reality (e.g., race, money, illness categories) is created and maintained by people, not naturally givenBerger & Luckmann"This is real because we collectively agree it is."

Also know rational choice / social exchange (behavior as cost–benefit maximization; reciprocity) and feminist theory (a conflict-oriented analysis centering gender).

The manifest vs. latent trap (Merton). Manifest functions are intended and recognized (school educates children). Latent functions are unintended but beneficial (school provides childcare, social networks). Latent dysfunctions are unintended and harmful. Don't confuse "latent" with "negative"—latent just means unintended.

Social institutions

  • Family: kinship; forms of marriage (monogamy, polygamy); nuclear vs. extended; functions of socialization and regulation.
  • Education: hidden curriculum (implicit norms/values taught alongside content), teacher expectancy effect, tracking, educational stratification and reproduction of inequality.
  • Religion: Durkheim's sacred/profane and social cohesion; Weber's Protestant ethic; religiosity; types—church, sect, cult/new religious movement; secularization; fundamentalism.
  • Government/economy: types of authority (Weber: traditional, charismatic, rational-legal); power; division of labor; capitalism vs. socialism.
  • Medicine (high-yield): the sick role (Parsons—the ill are exempted from normal duties but obligated to seek help and want to get well); medicalization (defining non-medical problems as medical, e.g., normal sadness → "disorder"); the illness experience; healthcare delivery; social epidemiology.

Culture

Material culture (physical objects) vs. symbolic/nonmaterial culture (beliefs, values, norms, language). Culture lag (nonmaterial culture trails material/technological change). Culture shock. Ethnocentrism vs. cultural relativism. Subculture (within, distinct) vs. counterculture (opposes the dominant). Assimilation vs. multiculturalism; cultural diffusion and transmission.

AAMC Content Category 9A, “Understanding social structure” (theoretical approaches: functionalism, conflict theory, symbolic interactionism, social constructionism, exchange–rational choice, feminist theory; social institutions; culture); Khan Academy MCAT: Society and Culture. Sick role: Parsons (1951); medicalization: Conrad.

Module 9 · Demographics, Stratification & Social Inequality

Demographics (Content Category 9B)

  • Demographic variables: age, gender, race/ethnicity, immigration status, sexual orientation. Distinguish sex (biological) from gender (social construct); race (socially constructed category) from ethnicity (shared culture).
  • Demographic structure: fertility, mortality, migration; population pyramids.
  • Demographic transition (high birth/death → low birth/death as societies industrialize); Malthusian theory.
  • Population dynamics: push/pull migration factors; urbanization, suburbanization, urban decline/renewal.
  • Social movements: proactive vs. reactive; relative deprivation; resource mobilization theory. Globalization and its social effects.

Social stratification & inequality (Content Category 10A)

ConceptDefinition
Social stratificationHierarchical ranking of people into layers (strata) with unequal access to resources
Caste vs. class systemCaste = closed, ascribed at birth, little mobility; class = relatively open, achievement possible
SESSocioeconomic status—composite of income, education, occupation
Cultural & social capitalBourdieu: nonfinancial assets (knowledge, tastes—cultural) and network/relationship resources (social) that confer advantage
IntersectionalityOverlapping identities (race, class, gender) compound disadvantage
Social mobilityIntergenerational (across generations) vs. intragenerational (within a lifetime); vertical vs. horizontal; meritocracy
PovertyAbsolute (below subsistence) vs. relative (below societal standard); social exclusion; the cycle of poverty
Spatial inequalityResidential segregation, environmental justice, neighborhood effects
Health and healthcare disparities (the section's punchline). Inequality by class, gender, and race produces unequal health outcomes (the "socioeconomic gradient in health"—lower SES, worse health) and unequal access to care. Institutionalized discrimination and barriers to institutional resources are the explicit AAMC framing. Expect passages connecting a sociological concept to a medical/epidemiological outcome.

Also high-yield: privilege, power, the difference between prejudice (attitude) and institutional/structural discrimination (built into systems), and the distinction between equality (same treatment) and equity (treatment proportional to need).

AAMC Content Categories 9B (“Demographic characteristics and processes”) and 10A (“Social inequality”); Khan Academy MCAT: Demographics & Social Inequalities. Cultural/social capital: Bourdieu; socioeconomic gradient in health is AAMC-named.
