1. Amino Acids, Proteins & Protein Structure

Proteins are polymers of 20 standard α-amino acids, each with an amino group, a carboxyl group, an H, and a variable side chain (R group) on the central α-carbon. Every standard amino acid except glycine (R = H) is chiral; nearly all are L-isomers (S configuration, with the exception of cysteine, which is R despite being L due to CIP priority of sulfur).

Side-chain classification (memorize these groupings)

ClassMembersKey test points
Nonpolar / hydrophobicGly, Ala, Val, Leu, Ile, Pro, Met, Phe, TrpBury in protein core; Pro kinks the backbone (secondary amine, no H-bond donor)
Polar unchargedSer, Thr, Cys, Asn, Gln, TyrSer/Thr/Tyr −OH are phosphorylation sites; Cys forms disulfides
Acidic (− at pH 7)Asp, GluCarboxyl side chains, low pKa (~3.7–4.3)
Basic (+ at pH 7)Lys, Arg, HisHis pKa ~6 → only one that buffers near physiological pH; key in enzyme active sites
Titration & pI The isoelectric point (pI) is the pH where net charge = 0. For an amino acid with no ionizable side chain, pI = average of the two pKa values (α-COOH ~2, α-NH3+ ~9 → pI ~5.5). For an acidic side chain, average the two lowest pKas; for a basic side chain, average the two highest. At pH < pI the molecule is net positive; at pH > pI it is net negative (this drives migration in electrophoresis).

The four levels of structure

LevelDefined byStabilizing interactions
PrimaryAmino acid sequence (peptide bonds)Covalent peptide (amide) bonds

Worked example A protein retains its molecular weight on SDS-PAGE under reducing conditions but runs as a single band at twice that weight on a native gel. What holds the dimer together? — SDS denatures and coats with negative charge (separating non-covalent contacts), and the reducing agent (β-mercaptoethanol/DTT) breaks disulfides. Since reduced SDS-PAGE shows monomers, the native dimer must be held by non-covalent quaternary interactions, not interchain disulfide bonds.

2. Enzymes: Kinetics & Regulation

Enzymes are biological catalysts that lower activation energy (Ea) without changing ΔG or the equilibrium position — they speed both forward and reverse reactions equally. They work by stabilizing the transition state (induced fit), not merely by binding substrate.

Michaelis–Menten kinetics

Rate v = Vmax[S] / (Km + [S]). Km = the [S] at half Vmax and approximates the inverse of binding affinity (low Km = high affinity). Vmax = kcat[Etotal] and depends on enzyme amount. The Lineweaver–Burk double-reciprocal plot (1/v vs 1/[S]) linearizes the curve: y-intercept = 1/Vmax, x-intercept = −1/Km, slope = Km/Vmax.

Inhibition — the classic exam table

High-yield trap On a Lineweaver–Burk plot, competitive inhibitors share the same y-intercept (Vmax unchanged) but increase the x-intercept magnitude shift (higher Km); noncompetitive inhibitors share the same x-intercept (Km unchanged) but raise the y-intercept (lower Vmax). Lines crossing on the y-axis = competitive; crossing on the x-axis = noncompetitive.

Regulation

  • Allosteric regulation: effectors bind sites other than the active site, shifting between tense (T, low-affinity) and relaxed (R, high-affinity) states — gives sigmoidal kinetics (cooperativity).
  • Feedback inhibition: end product inhibits an upstream committed-step enzyme (e.g., ATP and citrate inhibit phosphofructokinase-1).
  • Covalent modification: phosphorylation (kinases add, phosphatases remove) and proteolytic cleavage of zymogens (e.g., trypsinogen → trypsin).
  • Cofactors/coenzymes: metal ions and organic molecules (many from vitamins: NAD+/niacin, FAD/riboflavin, CoA/pantothenate).

AAMC, Biological & Biochemical Foundations — Content Category 1A & 1D (Principles of bioenergetics; enzyme structure/function and regulation); corroborated by Lehninger and Khan Academy MCAT: Enzyme kinetics.

3. Metabolism & Bioenergetics

Glucose oxidation is the central metabolic map. Know the inputs, outputs, location, and regulation of each stage.

StageLocationNet yield per glucoseRate-limiting enzyme
GlycolysisCytoplasm2 ATP, 2 NADH, 2 pyruvatePhosphofructokinase-1 (PFK-1)
Pyruvate dehydrogenaseMitochondrial matrix2 NADH, 2 CO2, 2 acetyl-CoAPDH (inhibited by acetyl-CoA, NADH)
Citric acid (TCA) cycleMatrixper turn: 3 NADH, 1 FADH2, 1 GTP, 2 CO2 (×2 = per glucose: 6 NADH, 2 FADH2, 2 GTP)Isocitrate dehydrogenase
Oxidative phosphorylationInner mitochondrial membrane~26–28 ATP (from ETC + chemiosmosis)Driven by proton gradient; O2 = final e− acceptor
Total ATP per glucose ≈ 30–32 Modern (consensus) yields use ~2.5 ATP/NADH and ~1.5 ATP/FADH2. Older texts cite 36–38 using 3 and 2. The variability comes from the shuttle used to move cytosolic NADH into the mitochondrion: the malate–aspartate shuttle preserves NADH (~2.5 ATP), while the glycerol-3-phosphate shuttle delivers electrons to FAD (~1.5 ATP).

Other fuel pathways to know

  • Fermentation: regenerates NAD+ anaerobically. Lactate (humans, via lactate dehydrogenase) or ethanol + CO2 (yeast). Net glycolytic yield stays 2 ATP.
  • Gluconeogenesis: not simply reverse glycolysis — bypasses the 3 irreversible steps (pyruvate carboxylase, PEPCK, FBPase-1, glucose-6-phosphatase). Occurs in liver/kidney.
  • Glycogen: synthesis via glycogen synthase, breakdown via glycogen phosphorylase (both under hormonal/phosphorylation control: glucagon/epinephrine → breakdown; insulin → storage).
  • β-oxidation: fatty acids → acetyl-CoA; far more ATP per gram than carbs (more reduced). Requires carnitine shuttle for long chains.
  • Pentose phosphate pathway: produces NADPH (reductive biosynthesis, glutathione) and ribose-5-phosphate (nucleotides); no ATP.
Worked example A poison blocks Complex IV (cytochrome c oxidase). What happens to the proton gradient and to NADH? — Electron flow halts at the end of the chain, so proton pumping stops and the gradient dissipates as ATP synthase keeps running briefly. With no terminal acceptor, NADH cannot be reoxidized, so NADH accumulates, the [NADH]/[NAD+] ratio rises, the TCA cycle stalls (NAD+-dependent dehydrogenases inhibited), and the cell shifts to anaerobic glycolysis (lactate ↑). Same logic applies to cyanide and CO.

4. Molecular Biology: Central Dogma & Gene Regulation

The central dogma: DNA → (transcription) → RNA → (translation) → protein, with reverse transcription (RNA → DNA) in retroviruses.

Replication, transcription, translation

  • Replication is semiconservative, bidirectional, 5'→3'. Leading strand continuous; lagging strand in Okazaki fragments. DNA polymerase needs a primer; has 3'→5' proofreading exonuclease activity.
  • Transcription (RNA polymerase II in eukaryotes) makes pre-mRNA. Eukaryotic processing: 5' 7-methylguanosine cap, 3' poly-A tail, and splicing out introns (spliceosome) keeping exons. Alternative splicing → multiple proteins from one gene.
  • Translation at the ribosome: codons read 5'→3'; the code is degenerate (multiple codons/amino acid), nearly universal, and non-overlapping. Start = AUG (Met); stops = UAA, UAG, UGA. tRNA wobble allows one tRNA to read multiple codons.

Biotech toolkit (high-yield) PCR amplifies DNA (denature–anneal–extend, Taq polymerase). Gel electrophoresis separates by size (DNA migrates to + anode, small fragments travel farther). Southern = DNA, Northern = RNA, Western = protein. Restriction enzymes cut at palindromes; CRISPR-Cas9 uses a guide RNA for targeted cuts. Sanger sequencing uses chain-terminating ddNTPs.

5. Genetics, Inheritance & Evolution

Mendelian genetics, probability, and population genetics show up as both discrete questions and pedigree/cross passages.

  • Mendel's laws: segregation (alleles separate in gametes) and independent assortment (different genes assort independently if unlinked).
  • Monohybrid cross (Aa × Aa): 3:1 phenotype, 1:2:1 genotype. Dihybrid (AaBb × AaBb): 9:3:3:1.
  • Non-Mendelian patterns: incomplete dominance (blended), codominance (both expressed, e.g., AB blood), pleiotropy, polygenic traits, epistasis (one gene masks another), and X-linkage.
  • Linkage & recombination: genes close together are inherited together; recombination frequency (% recombinants) estimates map distance (1% = 1 centimorgan).

Hardy–Weinberg equilibrium

p + q = 1  and  p2 + 2pq + q2 = 1 p, q = allele frequencies; p2 = homozygous dominant, 2pq = heterozygotes, q2 = homozygous recessive. Holds only under 5 assumptions: no mutation, no migration, no selection, random mating, and large population. If a recessive disease affects 1 in 10,000 (q2 = 0.0001), then q = 0.01, p ≈ 0.99, and carrier frequency 2pq ≈ 0.0198 (about 1 in 50).

Evolution

  • Natural selection acts on phenotypes; fitness = reproductive success. Types: directional, stabilizing, disruptive.
  • Other forces: genetic drift (random; strong in small populations — bottleneck, founder effect), gene flow, mutation (ultimate source of variation), non-random mating.
  • Speciation: reproductive isolation (allopatric = geographic; sympatric = same area). Species concept hinges on inability to produce viable, fertile offspring.

AAMC, Biological & Biochemical Foundations — Content Category 1C (Transmission of heritable information and processes that increase genetic diversity); corroborated by Campbell Biology and Khan Academy MCAT: Genetics.

6. Cell Biology, Membranes & Signaling

Eukaryotic cells compartmentalize function in membrane-bound organelles. The plasma membrane is a fluid mosaic phospholipid bilayer (hydrophilic heads out, hydrophobic tails in) studded with proteins; cholesterol buffers fluidity.

Organelle quick-reference

OrganelleFunction
Rough ERRibosomes; synthesis of secreted/membrane proteins
Smooth ERLipid synthesis, detoxification, Ca2+ storage
GolgiModifies, sorts, packages proteins (glycosylation)
LysosomeAcidic hydrolases; intracellular digestion (autophagy)
MitochondrionATP via oxidative phosphorylation; own DNA (maternal)
Peroxisomeβ-oxidation of very-long-chain fatty acids; breaks down H2O2

Membrane transport

  • Passive (no ATP, down gradient): simple diffusion (small nonpolar/gases), facilitated diffusion (channels/carriers for ions, glucose), osmosis (water).
  • Active (against gradient): primary uses ATP directly (Na+/K+-ATPase pumps 3 Na+ out / 2 K+ in); secondary/cotransport uses an existing ion gradient (symport/antiport).
  • Bulk: endocytosis (phagocytosis, pinocytosis, receptor-mediated) and exocytosis.
Signal transduction G-protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs): ligand binding → Gα exchanges GDP for GTP → activates adenylyl cyclase (cAMP) or phospholipase C (IP3/DAG → Ca2+ release). Receptor tyrosine kinases (RTKs): dimerize, autophosphorylate, trigger Ras–MAPK cascades (e.g., insulin, growth factors). Second messengers (cAMP, Ca2+, IP3) amplify the signal — one receptor → thousands of downstream molecules.

AAMC, Biological & Biochemical Foundations — Content Category 2A (Assemblies of molecules, cells, and groups of cells) & 3A; corroborated by Alberts Molecular Biology of the Cell and Khan Academy MCAT.

7. Microbiology: Prokaryotes & Viruses

Prokaryotes (bacteria, archaea) lack a nucleus and membrane-bound organelles; they have a single circular chromosome, 70S ribosomes, and often plasmids. Reproduction is asexual by binary fission, but genetic variation arises through three horizontal mechanisms:

MechanismHow DNA transfers
TransformationUptake of naked DNA from the environment
TransductionTransfer via bacteriophage (virus) vector
ConjugationDirect cell-to-cell via pilus; requires F (fertility) plasmid

Viruses

  • Acellular; obligate intracellular parasites — not alive by most definitions (no metabolism of their own). A virion = nucleic acid (DNA or RNA, never both) + protein capsid ± lipid envelope.
  • Lytic cycle: replicate immediately and lyse the host. Lysogenic cycle: integrate as a prophage, replicate passively, later switch to lytic.
  • Retroviruses (e.g., HIV): RNA genome → reverse transcriptase makes DNA → integrase inserts into host genome.
  • Prions (misfolded proteins) and viroids (naked RNA) are subviral infectious agents.
Worked example Why do antibiotics targeting the bacterial 70S ribosome (e.g., tetracyclines, aminoglycosides) generally spare human cells? — Human cytosolic ribosomes are 80S (40S + 60S subunits), structurally distinct from the bacterial 70S (30S + 50S). The drug selectively binds the prokaryotic subunit. (Note: human mitochondrial ribosomes resemble 70S, which is why some such antibiotics carry mitochondrial side effects — a favorite passage twist.)

AAMC, Biological & Biochemical Foundations — Content Category 2B (Structure, growth, physiology, and genetics of prokaryotes and viruses) & 2C; corroborated by Campbell Biology and Khan Academy MCAT: Microbiology.

8. Organ Systems & Physiology

Concept 3 (Category 3B) tests the integrative function of organ systems, especially homeostasis and feedback. The nervous and endocrine systems coordinate; the others execute.

SystemMust-know facts
NervousResting potential ~−70 mV (Na+/K+-ATPase + K+ leak). Action potential: depolarization (Na+ in) → repolarization (K+ out) → refractory period. Saltatory conduction at nodes of Ranvier. Synapse: Ca2+ influx → neurotransmitter release.
EndocrineHormones: peptides (surface receptors, fast, second messengers) vs steroids (intracellular receptors, slow, alter transcription). Negative feedback (e.g., hypothalamus–pituitary–thyroid axis). Insulin lowers / glucagon raises blood glucose.
CirculatoryHeart: SA node pacemaker → AV node → bundle of His → Purkinje. Pulmonary loop (right side, to lungs) vs systemic (left side). O2 dissociation curve: right shift (Bohr) with ↑CO2, ↑H+, ↑temp, ↑2,3-BPG.
RespiratoryGas exchange at alveoli; diaphragm contracts → inhalation (negative-pressure). CO2 mostly carried as bicarbonate; central chemoreceptors sense pH/CO2.
RenalNephron: filtration (glomerulus) → reabsorption (PCT) → secretion → concentration (loop of Henle countercurrent). ADH ↑ water reabsorption; aldosterone ↑ Na+ reabsorption.
DigestiveMechanical + chemical breakdown; zymogens activated in gut; most absorption in small intestine; liver processes nutrients (portal vein).
ImmuneInnate (fast, nonspecific) vs adaptive (specific, memory). B cells → antibodies (humoral); T cells: helper (CD4) coordinate, cytotoxic (CD8) kill. Clonal selection.
MusculoskeletalSliding filament: actin + myosin, Ca2+ binds troponin → tropomyosin shifts → cross-bridge cycling (ATP). Sarcomere shortens; A band constant, I band & H zone shrink.
ReproductiveSpermatogenesis (continuous) vs oogenesis (arrested). Menstrual cycle: FSH/LH → estrogen/progesterone; LH surge triggers ovulation.
Worked example During heavy exercise, why does hemoglobin release more O2 to muscle? — Active muscle produces CO2, H+ (lactic acid), and heat, and raises 2,3-BPG. All of these right-shift the oxygen–hemoglobin dissociation curve (the Bohr effect), lowering Hb's O2 affinity so it unloads O2 precisely where the tissue demand is highest.
